# Signal Cartography

A design philosophy for documenting audio and electrical signal flow as if drawing a transit map for the invisible.

## The Movement

Signal Cartography treats audio and electrical pathways the way Harry Beck treated the London Underground: not as literal photographic representations of where wires run, but as topological maps where the *relationships* between elements carry meaning. The diagram is not a snapshot of the studio. It is a portrait of how signal moves through it. Every line has weight. Every junction is intentional. Every color is a vocabulary, not a decoration.

The aesthetic borrows from three masters in equal measure. Harry Beck's spatial logic — geometric clarity over geographic accuracy. Massimo Vignelli's chromatic discipline — color as primary information, never ornament. Otl Aicher's typographic precision — type as quiet servant to the visual system, never shouting, never apologetic. The result is a document that reads like a museum-grade transit map, but maps current and waveforms instead of trains.

## Space and Form

The composition breathes. Generous negative space surrounds every device node, every connection line. Devices appear as restrained geometric forms — rectangles with the same proportions throughout, anchored to an invisible grid that aligns horizontally, vertically, and along key signal pathways. The grid is the discipline beneath the apparent ease. Nothing floats arbitrarily. Every node sits where the signal topology demands it sits.

Connection lines are straight, with right-angle turns only — never curves, never diagonals. This is signal hierarchy, not biological flow. The right-angle path says: *this signal was routed deliberately by a person who thought about it.* Lines do not cross unless they must, and when they must, the over-line bridges with a small arc — a quiet acknowledgment that two pathways share space but never touch electrically.

## Color and Material

Color is the vocabulary. Each connection type owns a single color and never borrows from another. Yellow speaks for analog audio — the warm, golden signal of vinyl and line-level. Green speaks for the digital audio pipe — USB carrying class-compliant samples. Teal speaks for USB-C data, the dock-to-workstation bidirectional spine. Blue speaks for video — DisplayPort and HDMI carrying pixels to the eye. Violet speaks for peripheral data — USB-A devices, MIDI, storage. Light gray speaks for power — AC and DC, present but never central.

The background is the deepest ink, near-black but not absolute, so the spectrum colors can sing without harshness. White is reserved for typography and the thinnest hairline accents — used like a violinist uses silence between notes. Every color choice is the product of master-level calibration: each spectrum hue was chosen specifically to remain distinguishable for any reader, including those with common color-vision differences, and to harmonize across the full palette without competing.

## Scale and Rhythm

Devices are sized by their role in the signal hierarchy, not by their physical size in the room. The audio interface — the MOTU M4, where analog becomes digital — is rendered slightly larger than its neighbors because it is the conceptual fulcrum of the entire diagram. The workstation, where all paths converge, sits at visual center. Peripheral nodes are smaller, quieter, deliberately less prominent. The rhythm of node sizes guides the eye through the signal flow the way a conductor guides a phrase.

## Typography

Type is whispered. Device names are set in a single weight of a clean geometric sans-serif, sized just large enough to read at presentation distance, never larger. Specifications and port numbers appear in a smaller weight, set in monospace to suggest the technical register of a service manual. The brand wordmark appears once, in the upper-left, set with the gravity of an editorial masthead. No text shouts. No text fills space it does not earn. Every label was placed by hand, then re-placed, then placed again, until it sat where only it could sit.

## Craftsmanship

This is a document that should look as if it took a master draughtsman three days to perfect. Every line weight calibrated. Every margin measured. Every color sampled from a palette chosen with the patience of a painter mixing oils. Every label set with the consideration of a book designer placing chapter titles. The viewer should be unable to find a place where it could be improved — because improvement was already pursued, then pursued again, until the work was returned to its quietest, most essential form. This is the work of someone at the top of their field, who understood that restraint is the final and hardest discipline.

Nothing decorative. Nothing arbitrary. Nothing accidental. Signal Cartography is the art of making the invisible inevitable.
